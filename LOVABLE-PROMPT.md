## RULE ONE — DO NOT INVENT ANYTHING

This is legal advertising. False or unverifiable claims about an attorney's
results, experience, or credentials create real regulatory exposure.

Everything factual you need is in this prompt. If a piece of information is not
in this prompt, **leave it out entirely** — do not fill the space with something
plausible. Specifically, you must NOT invent:

- Dollar amounts recovered, settlement or verdict figures, case counts, or win rates
- Years of experience or "X+ years in practice"
- Client testimonials, review counts, or star ratings
- Podcast appearances, panels, keynotes, speaking events, or TV segments
- Awards, honors, rankings, or bar association positions
- Books, courses, newsletters, or products
- Statistics of any kind

If a section you design would look better with a statistic, use one of the real
facts listed under "Credential facts" instead, or design the section so it does
not need a number. Do not use placeholder numbers like "500+ cases" even

## DESIGN — this is where I want you to be ambitious

Everything above is fixed. The design is entirely yours, and I want a genuinely
different take rather than a safe one.

**Do not default to a white background with blue accents.** That has already
been built. Go somewhere else. Some directions worth considering, though you
are not limited to these:

- A dark, editorial, high-contrast treatment
- Warm neutrals — bone, sand, oxblood, deep green, brass
- A bold monochrome with a single unexpected accent
- Something with real editorial or magazine structure
- Heavy, confident typography as the primary design element

Be equally free with structure. Reconsider what the sections are, how many pages
there should be, what the navigation does, how scroll behaves, how imagery is
cropped and framed. Asymmetry, oversized type, unusual grids, horizontal
scrolling sections, sticky elements — all welcome if they serve him.

Two things I care about more than any particular style: it should look like it
was designed for **this specific person**, and it should feel like the site of
someone at the top of his field. Make it memorable.

Requirements that hold regardless of direction: fully responsive, accessible
color contrast, fast, real semantic HTML, and a working contact form.
